# 25 Greatest Blue Oceans in History

**Pattern: Don't compete. Create a category where competition is irrelevant.**

---

## 1. CIRQUE DU SOLEIL (1984)

**Red Ocean:** Traditional circus
- Competing with Ringling Bros, Barnum & Bailey
- Animal acts, clowns, three rings
- Declining industry, high costs (animals), shrinking audience (kids)

**Blue Ocean:** "Circus for adults"
- No animals (cost savings)
- Artistic, theatrical, Broadway-quality
- Target: Adults who go to theater, not kids who go to circus
- Price: $100+ tickets (vs. $20 circus tickets)

**Result:** Created $6B company in "dying" industry by not competing with circuses

**Lesson:** When your industry is dying, don't fight harder. Create a new industry.

---

## 2. SOUTHWEST AIRLINES (1971)

**Red Ocean:** Airlines competing on routes, meals, first class
- Hub-and-spoke models
- Business travelers
- Premium service, high prices

**Blue Ocean:** "The bus in the sky"
- Point-to-point routes (no hubs)
- No meals, no first class, no assigned seats
- Target: People who would otherwise drive
- Price: Cheaper than driving

**Result:** Most profitable airline for 47 consecutive years

**Lesson:** Don't compete for existing customers. Create customers who didn't fly before.

## 3. NETFLIX (1997-2007)

**Blue Ocean #1 (1997):** DVD by mail
- Red Ocean: Blockbuster (late fees, limited inventory, drive to store)
- Blue Ocean: No late fees, unlimited selection, delivered to you
- Target: People who hated Blockbuster's late fees

**Blue Ocean #2 (2007):** Streaming
- Red Ocean: Cable TV (Comcast, Time Warner)
- Blue Ocean: On-demand, no commercials, binge-watching
- Target: People who hated commercials and fixed schedules

**Result:** $300B company. Blockbuster bankrupt. Cable TV declining.

**Lesson:** Blue ocean once, then do it again when competition catches up.

## 4. iPOD/iTUNES (2001)

**Red Ocean:** MP3 players (Diamond Rio, Creative Zen)
- Competing on storage size, features
- Piracy was the enemy

**Blue Ocean:** "1,000 songs in your pocket" + legal music store
- Not competing with MP3 players (they were clunky)
- Competing with piracy by making legal easier than illegal
- Target: Music lovers who wanted guilt-free convenience
- Price: $0.99/song (vs. free piracy but with hassle)

**Result:** Killed CD industry, killed piracy, created iPhone ecosystem

**Lesson:** Don't compete with free. Make legal more convenient than illegal.

## 5. UBER (2009)

**Red Ocean:** Taxis (medallions, dispatch, cash, uncertain arrival)
- Regulated, limited supply
- Bad customer experience

**Blue Ocean:** "Everyone can be a driver"
- Bypass medallion system (regulatory arbitrage)
- Target: People who hated waiting for taxis
- Price: Surge pricing (dynamic supply/demand)

**Result:** $150B company. Taxi industry disrupted globally.

**Lesson:** When regulation creates scarcity, create abundance.

## 9. NINTENDO WII (2006)

## 10. DOLLAR SHAVE CLUB (2011)

## THE BLUE OCEAN FORMULA

**Step 1: Identify the Red Ocean**
- Who are incumbents?
- What are they competing on?
- Who are they targeting?

**Step 2: Find the Non-Customers**
- Who DOESN'T buy from incumbents? Why not?
- What prevents them from buying?
- What would make them buy?

**Step 3: Create the Blue Ocean**
- Change the axis of competition
- Target the non-customers
- Make the old competition irrelevant

**Step 4: Dominate**
- Incumbents can't copy (would cannibalize existing business)
- You own the category
- First-mover advantage compounds

## WHY BLUE OCEANS WIN

**Red Ocean Strategy:**
- Fight for existing customers
- Compete on features/price
- Zero-sum game (steal market share)
- High marketing costs (convince people to switch)
- Incremental growth

**Blue Ocean Strategy:**
- Create new customers
- Compete on new axis (make old competition irrelevant)
- Positive-sum game (expand the market)
- Low marketing costs (customers find you because you solve unsolved problem)
- Exponential growth

**The Math:**
- Red Ocean: Fight 100 competitors for 10M customers = 100K customers each
- Blue Ocean: Own 1 category with 2M new customers = 2M customers (20× more)

## THE ANTI-PATTERN (Failed Blue Oceans)

### Why Some Blue Oceans Fail:

**Google Glass (2013):**
- Created new category (AR glasses for consumers)
- Problem: No one wanted the category (solution looking for problem)
- **Lesson:** Blue ocean must solve real pain, not invent imaginary need

**Quibi (2020):**
- Created new category ("quick bites" 10-min shows for mobile)
- Problem: YouTube already solved "video on mobile" better
- **Lesson:** Blue ocean must be better than existing alternatives

**Segway (2001):**
- Created new category (personal transportation)
- Problem: Walking is free, Segway is $5,000
- **Lesson:** Blue ocean must be 10× better, not 10% better

**MoviePass (2017):**
- Created new category (unlimited movies for $10/month)
- Problem: Economics didn't work (lost money on every customer)
- **Lesson:** Blue ocean must be profitable, not subsidized by VC
